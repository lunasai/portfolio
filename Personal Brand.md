### Keywords & glossary

Terms Luna owns — either coined by her, applied in a distinctive way, or central to her vocabulary. Definitions in her voice. Use these consistently; do not paraphrase into generic alternatives.

**Brand volume**
A framework I introduced to guide teams on how much brand expression to apply in any given context — from bold, emotional moments at high volume to functional, efficient flows at low volume. It replaced subjective debates about "how branded should this be?" with a shared decision-making tool. Invented for Heineken's eazle; applicable to any product team managing brand in a system.

**Design Jam**
A full-day innovation session where designers explore how the brand lives in real use cases — not in theory, not in a workshop deck. At Heineken, it started as a one-off experiment and became a quarterly ritual across the organization. The format works because it gives people a story to be part of, not a mandate to follow.

**Federated adoption**
The model where a small, expert design system team (core) enables a large community (contributors, ambassadors, observers) to own quality alongside them — rather than bottlenecking everything through a central gatekeeper. At Crate: 6 dedicated DS → 235 community at ~39:1 leverage. The goal is not more contributors; it is better infrastructure so the right contributors can move confidently.

**Crate stickers**
Physical/digital artifacts created to give the design system a cultural identity — building recognition and community feeling so the system becomes part of team identity, not just a Figma library. Detail: the stickers are a signal that Luna thinks about adoption as a cultural problem, not a documentation problem.

**Cohesion across complex ecosystems**
Her phrase for what a design system actually does when it is working: not just visual consistency, but organizational alignment — teams with different incentives, timelines, and geographies arriving at experiences that feel like they came from one place.

**Chapters**
The structural unit Luna uses for case studies. Not "phases" or "deliverables" — chapters. The word is intentional: it frames work as a story with stakes, not a process with outputs.

### Metrics usage guidelines

**Rule 1: Human outcome first, number second.**
Never open with the number. Open with what it means for a person or team.
- *Wrong:* "75% of users reported better collaboration."
- *Right:* "Teams describe the biggest change as how they work together — 75% highlight better collaboration as the top outcome."

**Rule 2: Cluster metrics, don't scatter them.**
Group related numbers into a single proof moment rather than placing one metric per paragraph. A cluster of three numbers in a callout panel reads as evidence; one number per paragraph reads as padding.

**Rule 3: Provide context for every number.**
A number without a reference point is decoration. "70 SUS score" means nothing without "above the industry standard of 68, maintained while doubling team size."

**Rule 4: Use the ⚡️ / 👉 marker to signal the clincher.**
In deck format, the lightning bolt signals the non-negotiable outcome of the story. On the web or in LinkedIn posts, use bold + a line break. The marker is the visual grammar of "this is what you need to remember."

**Rule 5: Metrics about people beat metrics about systems.**
"2,600 design × dev errors prevented per year" is good. "867 hours of designer capacity freed — the equivalent of five months" is better. Always translate system metrics into time, money, or human effort when possible.

## 7. Visual Direction

*This section is based on a live read of the FigJam moodboard (fileKey: EOTEGEJUeediSdV3BZm5Ng, read 2026-04-13) via Figma MCP. All visual elements described below were confirmed on the board at the time of writing.*

### Creative concept

**Organic + synthetic** — the tension between natural warmth and systematic precision, held in one place without resolving into either.

In practice: her brand should feel like something grown and something built coexist in the same frame. Not "techy designer" and not "ethereal artist" — both, at the same time.

---

### Color & contrast

**Direction:**
- **Anchor in deep warm tones.** The Suno reference on the moodboard — deep magenta rolling into warm copper-orange — is the temperature reference. This is not a purple-tech palette; it is warm, almost sunset, almost biological.
- **Grain over flat.** No color in this visual system should read as flat fill. Film grain, noise, or texture should be layered over primary tones. The grain is the signature.
- **High-contrast black-and-white moments.** The retro Mac and "72" reference on the board signal that full desaturation — B/W — is a legitimate mode, not a fallback. Use it for analytical or typographic-dominant moments.
- **One bold accent.** The pixel red apple on the board: one hyper-saturated moment of color against a neutral or dark ground. Not a palette, a punctuation mark.
- **Soft atmospheric midtones.** The misty forest/laptop image and the pastoral green landscape provide the quiet register — where the depth lives before the grain hits.

**What to avoid:** Pastel palettes. Blue-dominant tech gradients. "Minimal white with one accent color" portfolios. Anything that looks like Linear, Notion, or a Webflow template.

---

### Typography

**Direction:**
- **Editorial serif + technical sans.** The FLORA AI reference uses an elegant serif headline against clean body text. The "72" and "ALMOST THERE" references show that large, spare numerics and clean sans-serif have a role in the system alongside the more expressive serif.
- **Name as anchor.** The moodboard includes "Luna" set large in what appears to be a humanist display typeface — the name is a logotype, not just a label. In any layout, her name should be set with typographic intention, not defaulted.
- **Restraint in hierarchy.** Not many type sizes. Two or three levels, clearly differentiated. The confidence is in what is *not* on the page.
- **Personality markers.** The chapter-structure of her case studies — "Chapter One," "Chapter Two" — benefits from a serif at a larger size. The metrics callouts benefit from the technical sans, set tight.

**Pairing personality:** Display/editorial serif (humanist, slightly warm — think Canela, Freight Display, or Domaine) paired with a geometric or humanist sans for body and data (Söhne, Inter, or ABC Diatype register). Not Helvetica. Not Circular. Nothing that reads as Swiss-neutral.

---

### Imagery & texture

**Direction, traced to specific moodboard elements:**

- **Film grain everywhere.** The Suno purple image is the texture reference — heavy, analog grain over a rich gradient. This is not the subtle noise of a CSS filter; it is a material choice. Grain = warmth + depth.
- **Botanical macro.** The FLORA AI image (flowers in shallow depth-of-field, geometric marks overlaid) is the image style reference for organic moments: real nature, photographed with care, slightly abstracted by a grid or mark system.
- **Dither / pixel art as nostalgia, not retro kitsch.** The Mac and apple pixel art read as genuine affection for computing history — not ironic retro. Use dithered or pixelated textures in moments of levity or in "system/infrastructure" contexts where the tech-nostalgia feels earned.
- **Halo & glow.** The misty laptop image: a precise object (laptop, screen) emitting soft light in an atmospheric, soft-focus environment. This effect — something technical glowing in something organic — is the visual metaphor for her positioning. Use in hero images, section breaks, or any moment that needs to feel significant without being loud.
- **Morphing / abstract forms.** The fluid, abstract digital paintings on the right of the board are the motion reference — if there is animation or interactive work, it should feel like form shifting, not elements entering and exiting on a timeline.
- **Cartographic precision.** The technical diagram (dotted lines, dense information) on the far right is the analytical register. Reserve for content about systems, architecture, process — but make it beautiful; do not default to plain wireframes.

---

### Layout & composition

**Direction:**
- **Structure with breathing room.** The moodboard does not cluster everything to the center. Images are placed with tension — some overlapping, some isolated. Layouts should have clear structure *and* deliberate empty space.
- **Name as visual anchor.** "Luna" appears large on the board as a standalone typographic element. Her name is not a label in a header nav — it is a design object that commands space.
- **Asymmetry within a grid.** The "Group 2" cluster on the board (three images of different sizes stacked into a tight frame) shows that she gravitates toward asymmetric compositions that still feel organized. Not a strict column grid, but not chaos.
- **Spare numerical moments.** "72" and "ALMOST THERE" on the board — single large numbers or short phrases given full composition weight. In portfolio layouts, a metric can be a layout element, not just a caption.
- **Layers of depth.** Multiple images on the board overlap or sit in close proximity without merging. The visual system should feel like it has layers — foreground, middle, atmospheric background — rather than flat panels side by side.

### Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Layer grain texture over any rich color | Use flat fills as primary backgrounds |
| Set her name large — it is a visual anchor | Tuck her name into a nav bar in small caps |
| Use one hyper-saturated accent color as punctuation | Build an entire palette from multiple saturated colors |
| Pair an editorial serif with a technical sans | Use one typeface for everything (signals no typographic point of view) |
| Use botanical photography with geometric marks overlaid | Use stock photography of people looking at laptops |
| Allow atmospheric depth (blur/halo) in hero moments | Make every image sharp and equally legible |
| Give a metric its own typographic moment ("72", large) | List metrics in a bullet run inside a paragraph |
| Use dither/pixel texture in system/infrastructure contexts | Apply pixel aesthetic decoratively without the earned nostalgia |
| Let the layout breathe — asymmetry within a grid | Fill every container edge-to-edge |
| Reference the Escher quote visually (order from chaos) | Use generic "minimal portfolio" white space as a style |
